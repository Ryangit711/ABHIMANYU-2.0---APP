### Category 2: Change Management Fundamentals (ADKAR/Kotter Fluency)

**Q4: "Walk us through your approach to change management."**

> *"I use ADKAR as the diagnostic framework and Kotter as the execution engine. Every initiative starts with Awareness — why is this change necessary? I build that through town halls, direct leadership communication, and one-on-ones. Then Desire — helping people see what's in it for them. Then Knowledge — hands-on training, job aids, peer mentoring. Ability — supported practice with real systems. Reinforcement — measurement, recognition, course correction. On a recent digital transformation, this structure got us 92% adoption across 32 locations within 12 months."*

**Q5: "How do you handle resistance?"**

> *"Resistance is information. When clinicians resisted our new digital systems, I didn't push harder — I listened. Ran focus groups, identified the root causes: fear of slower workflows during the learning curve, concern about losing autonomy. We addressed each specifically — created super-user peer mentors, adjusted rollout pace for low-confidence locations, showed early wins at pilot sites. Resistance dropped from 60%+ to under 15% within 6 months. The key is: resistance isn't the enemy. Unaddressed resistance is."*

**Q6: "How do you measure change success?"**

> *"Three tiers. Tier 1 — Adoption metrics: logins, transactions moved to new system, error rates. Tier 2 — Sentiment: employee surveys, resistance tracking, focus group feedback. Tier 3 — Business outcomes: revenue recovery, efficiency gains, retention rates. I build KPI dashboards that track all three in real time — I did this across 32 locations and reduced reporting lag by 30%. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it."*

**Q7: "Tell me about a change initiative that failed or underperformed."**

> *"Early in my career, I rolled out a new scheduling system and focused almost entirely on the technology — training on how to use the software. We hit 70% adoption and stalled. What I missed was the Desire layer of ADKAR. The staff didn't understand why the old system was being replaced. Once we went back, explained the rationale, showed them how it made their jobs easier, and addressed their specific concerns, adoption climbed to 92%. That lesson shaped my entire approach — technology is 20% of change. The human side is 80%."*

### Category 3: Behavioral / STAR Questions (CM-Framed)

**Q8: "Tell me about a time you managed a large-scale transformation."**

> *"Situation: Our company acquired 5 clinic groups over 18 months, adding 32 locations across 4 states. Task: Integrate all operations — clinical, billing, scheduling — without disrupting patient care. Action: I designed an ADKAR-based integration playbook with Day 1/30/100 milestones. Each acquisition had a dedicated change workstream: stakeholder mapping, communication plan, training curriculum, KPI dashboard. I personally coached 70+ individuals through the transition. Result: 100% talent retention across all 5 acquisitions. Zero operational disruption. 92% digital adoption within 12 months."*

**Q9: "Describe a time you influenced stakeholders who didn't report to you."**

> *"I needed 12 department heads across 5 clinic groups to align on a shared operational framework. None reported to me. I built a cross-functional steering committee with rotating chair, used data to show each group how the framework solved their specific pain points, and found a champion in the most skeptical department head first. Once he adopted, the rest followed. We maintained that alignment for 5 consecutive years through quarterly OKR reviews."*

**Q10: "How do you approach training and capability building?"**

> *"Training isn't a one-time event — it's a system. On our digital rollout across 32 locations, I designed a multi-layer approach: (1) train-the-trainer for super-users at each location, (2) hands-on workshops for all end users, (3) one-on-one coaching for resistant individuals, (4) drop-in support clinics for 3 months post-launch. We also created video job aids and quick-reference guides. The super-user model was the game-changer — peers teaching peers had 3x better adoption than manager-led training."*

**Q11: "Tell me about a time you used data to drive change."**

> *"During our digital transformation, I built real-time KPI dashboards tracking adoption by location, module, and user role. When a location showed low adoption in week 2, I could identify it immediately and deploy targeted intervention — additional training, on-site support, leadership visit. This data-driven approach reduced reporting lag by 30% and let us shift from reactive to proactive change management. The dashboard also helped leadership see which changes were sticking and which needed reinforcement."*

**Q12: "How do you manage competing priorities during a transformation?"**

> *"You can't boil the ocean. When we were integrating 5 acquisitions simultaneously with a digital transformation running in parallel, I prioritized by impact and dependency. Clinical systems first (patient-facing), billing second (revenue), scheduling third (operations). Each had a separate change workstream with its own timeline, milestones, and success metrics. Weekly war room meetings with workstream leads tracked progress and flagged blockers. The key was sequencing — some changes depend on others. Get the order right and everything flows."*
